Observer Focus
Regardless of lesson type — lecture, Socratic, or discussion — the core question is the same: who is doing the intellectual work? Are students active participants or passive recipients?
Specific Look-Fors
▸Who is talking more — teacher or students? Even in a lecture, some student voice should be present.
▸Is the teacher spreading participation intentionally — or defaulting to the same willing students?
▸Is the teacher giving students time to think — or filling every silence?
▸Watch for learned helplessness — students who wait for the teacher to answer rather than attempting themselves.

Choose One Tally to Track

Pick one tally to focus on during this visit — not all three. Trying to track everything at once pulls your attention away from the room. Over multiple visits, each tally gives you a different layer of data about this teacher's practice.

Option A — Student Participation
Who is speaking and how often. Use this when you want to know whether participation is spread across the room or carried by a few students.
Add student names above, then tap their count each time they speak or respond.
Option B — Response Type
What modes is the teacher using. Use this when you want to know whether the teacher is varying how students respond or defaulting to one approach.
Option C — Teacher vs. Student Talk
Who is carrying the conversation. Use this when you want a broad read on whether students are doing the intellectual work or the teacher is.

Every 5 minutes, tap who was primarily talking during that interval. Tap again to deselect.

Even in a strong lecture, a classroom with zero student voice across 20 minutes is worth exploring in the debrief.

Variety of Students Called On

Select the option that best describes what you observed. This is separate from your tally — it's your overall impression of how broadly the teacher spread participation.

Teacher gave students adequate time to think before expecting a response
Examples: asked a question and waited — did not fill the silence, did not call on the first hand immediately, allowed students to sit with the question before responding
⚠ Teacher answered their own questions before students had time to respond
Examples: asked a question, waited less than 3 seconds, then answered it themselves — this trains students to wait because the teacher will always fill the silence
