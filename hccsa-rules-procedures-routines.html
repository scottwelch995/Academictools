Rules, Procedures & Routines
What we establish, how we teach it, and why it matters for our students
The Central Insight
The number one problem in most classrooms is not discipline — it is the absence of clearly taught procedures. Most behavior problems are not defiance problems. They are procedure problems.
Three Distinct Concepts

These three things are related but not the same. Confusing them leads to classroom management that is reactive, exhausting, and inconsistent.

Discipline Rules
General standards for moral behavior in the classroom and school community.
  • Reactive — invoked when broken
  • Govern conduct and character
  • Breaking a rule carries a consequence
  • Should be few in number
  • Must be 100% consistently enforced
  • Apply at all times, in all situations
Rules define the moral boundaries of the community. They are non-negotiable.
Discipleship Procedures
The specific methods and processes for how things are done in your classroom.
  • Proactive — taught before they are needed
  • Show students how things are done
  • Breaking a procedure leads to retraining
  • Every recurring activity needs one
  • Taught explicitly — not assumed
  • Make the classroom organized and effective
Procedures are discipleship. You are training students in the habits of a learner.
Rhythm Routines
The habitual rhythms and structures that define how your classroom flows — not triggered by a student need, but built into the shape of the day.
  • Intentional — chosen and established by the teacher
  • Consistent — happen the same way, every time
  • Define the culture and feel of the classroom
  • Vary between classrooms, subjects, and teachers
  • Taught and reinforced until they are simply "how we do things here"
  • Free students to focus on learning, not logistics
Routines are not responses to student needs — they are the recurring shape of your classroom. They should be chosen with intention.

The foundation of the discipleship and discipline framework
The Framework in Practice

Discipline and discipleship are not opposites — they are two sides of the same commitment to the student. Everything flows from love and returns to fellowship.

★
Culture of Love & Mutual Respect
Everything begins and ends here. The teacher genuinely wants every student to succeed. This is not a technique — it is the foundation the entire framework rests on.
1
Teach
Instruct students clearly on rules, procedures, and expectations. State them, explain them, model them. Never assume students know what you have not explicitly taught.
Psalm 34:11 — "Come, children, listen to me; I will teach you the fear of the Lord."
2
Train
Practice the procedure until it becomes routine. Use roleplay, scenarios, and repetition. Behaviors must be rehearsed — not just explained — to become habits.
Ephesians 6:4 — "Bring them up in the training and instruction of the Lord."
3
Admonish
When disobedience occurs, correct gently and clearly. The expectation was taught, so the correction is fair. Admonish without contempt — the student's dignity must remain intact.
Galatians 6:1 — "Restore them gently."
4
Seek Support
When admonishment is insufficient, involve administration and families. You do not manage this alone. The school community supports the teacher in maintaining a well-ordered classroom.
5
Restore
The goal was never punishment — it was always restoration. Restore the student to fellowship with the teacher, the class, and the community. Then return to the positive cycle.
How to Teach a Procedure
1
Explain State the procedure clearly. Model it. Show students exactly what it looks like when done correctly. Explain why it matters.
2
Rehearse Practice it together under your supervision. Correct and redirect. Do not move on until students can do it correctly.
3
Reinforce Reteach, practice again, and affirm correct behavior. Repeat until the procedure becomes automatic — until it becomes a routine.

Procedures Every Teacher Should Establish

Before students arrive, identify and prepare to teach a procedure for each recurring student need. If you have not taught the procedure, you cannot hold students accountable for not following it.

Entering the Classroom
What do students do the moment they walk in? Where do they go? What do they begin? This sets the tone for the entire class period.
Getting the Teacher's Attention
How does a student signal they need help without disrupting the class? Raised hand, a card on the desk, waiting quietly — teach it explicitly.
Handing In Work
Where does work go? When? In what form? A clear procedure here eliminates dozens of interruptions and questions over the course of a year.
Student Needs During Class
Getting a Kleenex, throwing something away, sharpening a pencil, asking to use the restroom — each needs a clear, quiet procedure so learning is not interrupted.
Returning After Absence
How does a student get what they missed? Where do they look? Who do they ask first? A procedure puts the responsibility on the student, not the teacher.
Dismissal
How does class end? What must be done before students leave? The teacher — not the bell — dismisses the class. Teach this from day one.
Routines to Consider Building

Routines are not responses to student needs — they are the intentional rhythms that shape your classroom culture. They vary between teachers, subjects, and grade levels. The examples below are starting points, not prescriptions. Choose routines that reflect your values and your students.

Opening Routines
  • Bible reading or devotional to open class
  • Memory recitation — Scripture, poetry, Latin paradigm
  • A posted warm-up students begin immediately upon entering
  • Prayer before instruction begins
  • A brief review of the previous lesson
Class Rhythms
  • How discussion is opened and closed
  • How the teacher signals a shift in activity
  • Standing when a visitor enters the room
  • How students participate — when to speak, when to listen
  • How the class handles disagreement or debate
Closing Routines
  • A summary question — "what did we learn today?"
  • Time for students to write down assignments
  • A closing prayer or benediction
  • A consistent dismissal signal and process
  • A moment of reflection before leaving
Remember: A routine that exists only in the teacher's head is not yet a routine. It becomes one when students can do it without being told.
