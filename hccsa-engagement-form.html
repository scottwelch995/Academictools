Drop-In Observation · 20 Minutes · Internal Only
Engagement & Attention
Are students genuinely engaged — and is the teacher drawing them in?
CTEs Classroom Culture — Engagement (including attention as precondition) · Classroom Culture — Fellowship
Dispositions Joy · High Engagement · Conscientiousness · Personal Regard
Seven Laws Law 2 — The Law of the Learner: the teacher must secure and hold student attention before engagement is possible
Observer Focus
Are students genuinely engaged with the lesson, or just present? Is the teacher actively securing and holding attention — and drawing students into a love for what they're learning?
Specific Look-Fors
▸Attention comes before engagement. Are students first attending — eyes up, posture upright, minds present?
▸Is the lesson itself engaging, or does engagement depend entirely on the teacher's personality?
▸Is the teacher noticing drift and responding — or missing it?
▸Phones, open laptops, and side conversations are the visible evidence of lost attention — note them specifically.

Student Attention & Presence

Select Yes, No, or Not Observable for positive items. No opens an evidence box. For specific concerns, Yes opens an evidence box.

Positive Observations
Students appeared attentive — eyes tracking, not visibly distracted
Examples: eyes on the teacher or on whoever is speaking, not looking around, not staring blankly, not checking devices
Students demonstrated attentive posture — upright, leaning in, not slouched or leaning back
Examples: sitting up straight, leaning forward during discussion, bodies oriented toward the teacher or toward whoever is speaking
Teacher addressed visible disengagement
Examples: paused and waited, moved toward a distracted student, changed pace or tone, made direct eye contact, called a name gently, shifted the activity to re-engage
Specific Concerns
Were phones visible or in use during instruction?
Were laptops used for off-task activity?
Were there distracting side conversations during instruction?
Teacher Joy & Investment

Select Yes, No, or Not Observable for each item. Yes and No open an evidence box — write a specific moment, name, or quote. Not Observable means the lesson type didn't create a clear opportunity to see this.

Teacher appeared to genuinely enjoy the lesson
Examples: smiling naturally, animated voice, leaning in, unhurried delight in the content, visible enthusiasm for the material
Teacher's love for the subject was visible to students
Examples: shared why this topic matters personally, went deeper than required, lit up when discussing content, referenced their own wonder at the subject
Teacher demonstrated genuine personal regard for students — wanted them there, wanted them to succeed
Examples: used student names warmly, made eye contact, referenced something personal about a student, expressed excitement for a student's growth, corrected without contempt
Teacher was actively drawing students into loving what they were learning
Examples: connected content to students' lives, expressed genuine curiosity openly, designed a task students actually wanted to do, shared personal wonder at the material
Most Important Observation
Describe one specific moment where student attention was either won or lost. What did the teacher do — or not do — that made the difference?
Overall Impression
Your honest read — not a final evaluation. This informs your coaching conversation, not the teacher's file.
Debrief Coaching Question
"If you could replay one moment from today's lesson, which moment would you want to do differently — and what would you do instead?"
